INTRODUCTION

The black bear harvest report presents black bear harvest and hunter effort trends across the state
of Utah, based on data collected from bear hunters and compiled by the Utah Division of
Wildlife Resources (Division). This report represents the black bear portion of Federal Aid
Project W-65-M, segments 72 and 73, grant agreement periods July 1, 2023 through June 30,
2024 and July 1, 2024 through June 30, 2025, respectively. This Federal Aid project covers the
2024 black bear hunting seasons. This report presents black bear statewide trends since the
1960s, as well as population trend indicators.

This annual report presents the most complete and accurate summary of black bear harvest data
available at the time of publication. Occasionally, we will adjust previous years’ harvest numbers
in a subsequent report if more data came available after publication. This is typically the case
with counts of depredation (Wildlife Services/UDAF removal) mortality, and only rarely will
counts of hunter-harvest need adjustment.

METHODS

In addition to its traditional limited-entry hunts, Utah offered two additional types of bear hunts
in 2024: spot-and-stalk-only hunts, where hunters could not use dogs or bait; and harvest-
objective hunts, where an unlimited number of hunters could hunt in a harvest-objective unit
until the quota was met or the season ended. Baiting was not allowed during harvest-objective
hunts, and the use of dogs was also not allowed during most fall harvest-objective seasons.

Black bear pursuit was open in all 24 bear units. Three units (Book Cliffs, La Sal, and San Juan)
were closed to general black bear pursuit during the summer and only pursuers who applied for
and obtained a restricted pursuit permit were allowed to pursue in one of those units. Lists of
bear units, season dates, rules and regulations for the 2024 black bear hunting and pursuit
seasons were published in the 2024 Utah Black Bear Guidebook.

All successful hunters were required to have their bear checked at a Division office or by an
officer within 48 hours of harvest. At that time, Division personnel recorded harvest information,
collected tooth samples, and affixed a permanent tag to the pelt. Collected bear teeth were sent to
an independent lab where they sectioned the teeth and counted cementum annuli to estimate age
of each harvested bear. Bears aged to be 5-yrs or older were considered adults for the purposes of
calculating percent adult males in the harvest. Bear pursuit information was obtained through a
telephone survey of a statistically valid, random sample of bear pursuit permit holders. No data
were collected for the fall pursuit season.

Harvest data from depredating black bears came from damage reports received from the Utah
Department of Agriculture (UDAF) as well as from bears taken by Division personnel. Prior to
2023, USDA-APHIS Wildlife Services provided all depredation services and reports. Livestock
operators were authorized to take damaging bears and were required to have the carcass checked
by a Division officer within 48 hours. Harvest information was collected at that time. Other
mortality includes roadkill, poaching, found dead, etc.

RESULTS

In 2024, the Division sold 771 limited-entry black bear permits, 41 conservation permits, and
975 resident and 65 nonresident harvest-objective permits. Hunters harvested 407 bears, 113
taken in the spring, 168 in summer, and 126 in the fall. 39% of permits/quota were filled. Four
fall harvest-objective units (Cache/Ogden, Chalk Creek/East/Canyon/Morgan-South Rich early
fall season, La Sal, and Nebo) filled their harvest objectives in 2024, but only the Chalk
Creek/East/Canyon/Morgan-South Rich (early fall season) and La Sal closed early. The percent
of females in the harvest decreased from 42% in 2023 to 32% in 2024. 61 bears were killed or
died as the result of depredation or other mortality.

Utah issued 549 black bear pursuit permits in 2024. 105 permits were sold through a drawing
system and were valid for one restricted summer pursuit unit/season, while 6 nonresident permits
were sold through a drawing system and were valid for a single restricted spring pursuit unit.
These restricted permits were also valid on all other unrestricted units and seasons. 183 pursuers
went afield during the spring pursuit season and 128 pursuers pursued in unrestricted units
during the summer pursuit season. Zero nonresident pursuers pursued in restricted units during
the restricted spring pursuit season, although this projection was based on a very small sample of
3 of the 6 permit holders. 46 pursuers pursued in restricted units during the restricted summer
pursuit seasons. During the spring season, pursuers treed 0.3 bears per day, and pursuers treed
0.4 bears per day during the general summer season.
